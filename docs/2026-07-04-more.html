Models breaking

Webb's universe doesn't fit
Quanta Magazine

JWST keeps finding 'little red dots' — hundreds of objects that appeared 650 million years after the Big Bang and simply weren't predicted to exist. Leading hypothesis: a new class of object called a black hole star, where a supermassive black hole is so completely cocooned in dense gas that the gas itself emits light like a stellar atmosphere. The model isn't broken in one place — black holes too large, galaxies too mature, luminosities that don't follow the rules — and the theorists are now in a race where several incompatible explanations each fit some of the data.

We've Been Using the Wrong Science in Court for 50 Years
Veritasium

Veritasium goes long on how forensic disciplines — bite marks, hair analysis, blood spatter — were admitted as expert testimony for decades before anyone checked whether they actually worked. The uncomfortable core: the confidence of an expert witness and the validity of the underlying science are almost completely uncorrelated, and the institutions that should have caught this had every incentive not to look.

Infrastructure, visible and invisible

Finland pulls the plug on copper after 150 years
Euronews

On Tuesday, Elisa — Finland's last major copper-wire operator — held a ceremonial final call between its CEO and the country's telecom regulator, who said their goodbyes with a casual 'kuulemiin' (speak later) before ending 150 years of analogue infrastructure. Finland is the latest after Estonia, Norway, the Netherlands, and Spain; the country that gave the world Nokia is the one closing the chapter on the technology Nokia helped displace. There's something worth sitting with in the fact that a communications medium that reshaped the 20th century ended with two executives chatting about their teenage memories of it.

Prompt injection through a YouTube comment
javoriuski.com

A researcher discovered that YouTube Studio's AI comment-summarizer could be hijacked by leaving a comment containing instructions rather than feedback — the AI would faithfully execute them, prepending fake official notices to its output that creators would read as coming from YouTube itself. The stealth escalation: post a normal 'Nice video!' comment to avoid suspicion, then silently edit it to contain the payload later, since YouTube never re-notifies creators about comment edits. It's a clean illustration of what happens when an AI that reads untrusted input is given a voice of authority inside a trusted interface.

$200,000 to liberate Google's book scans
Anna's Archive

Anna's Archive has posted a $200k open bounty for a method that extracts Google Books' full scan collection at scale — books currently visible only as search snippets despite having been fully digitized. The bounty explicitly acknowledges that for a Google employee, $200k is not the draw: 'you'd be hailed a legendary archivist.' The framing is interesting — this is a civilizational memory problem dressed as a security research problem, and the line between the two is genuinely unclear.

Biology, aging, access

Old blood stem cells made young again — in mice
ScienceDaily / Cell Stem Cell

Mount Sinai researchers identified lysosomal overactivity — the cell's recycling centers going into overdrive — as a primary driver of blood stem cell aging, then reversed it in mice, restoring youthful regenerative capacity and balanced immune cell production. Published in Cell Stem Cell, so peer-reviewed and not a press release in a vacuum, though the mouse-to-human translation gap is real and the mechanism hasn't been tested in humans. If the lysosomal hypothesis holds, it would mean aging in the blood and immune system isn't just entropy — it's a specific, addressable dysfunction.

BPC-157, TB-500, Semax: the FDA decides July 23–24
Amanecia Health

Seven peptides including BPC-157, TB-500, Semax, and Epitalon came off the FDA's Category 2 'do not compound' list in April after the companies that originally nominated them withdrew those nominations — and the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meets July 23–24 to decide whether to formally clear them for compounding. The key nuance the coverage tends to bury: removal from the restricted list and FDA approval are different things, and how these substances are accessed (compounding pharmacy, prescription, oversight) still determines legality. Three weeks out from a concrete regulatory fork.

Off the map

The room got dumb before anyone noticed
Mike Bowler's blog

A closed meeting room with a few people in it crosses 1,000 ppm CO₂ within the first hour — the threshold at which Lawrence Berkeley Lab researchers measured significant decline in six of nine decision-making metrics versus a 600 ppm baseline; at 2,500 ppm (easily reached in a full all-day offsite), seven of nine fell into what they called 'dysfunctional' range. The author watched a real monitor hit 2,143 ppm. The specific cruelty: the domains that degrade first — strategy, planning, using information under pressure — are exactly the ones you called the meeting to exercise, and nobody in the room feels impaired, just a little foggy.
