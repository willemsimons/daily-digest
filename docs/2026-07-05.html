Making things at the edge

Fab2: Mass-Producing the Factory Itself
Tom's Hardware

Jim Keller's Atomic Semi has rebranded as Fab2 and shifted its entire premise: instead of making chips, it makes fabs — small, software-defined semiconductor factories designed to be manufactured in quantity. Sam Zeloof proved the concept by fabricating lithographic chips down to ~300nm in his parents' garage as a teenager. If a fab becomes a product you order rather than a cathedral you build, the geopolitics of who can make things physically starts to look very different.

A World Map in 445 Bytes
Simon Willison's Weblog

Iwo Kadziela compressed a recognizable ASCII world map into 445 bytes of deflate-raw data, decoded entirely in the browser using a fetch() call against a data: URI piped through the native DecompressionStream API — a trick most JavaScript developers didn't know was possible. It's constraint art that doubles as a cartography toy: the entire planet as a lookup table smaller than a tweet. The kind of thing that makes you want to open a canvas and start counting.

Ownership, forks, and what communities do when they're lied to

The CoMaps Fork: When 'Community-Driven' Wasn't
It's FOSS

Organic Maps — the beloved open-source, offline-first Google Maps alternative — turned out to have a small shareholder group making financial decisions without contributor input, including using donation funds for personal expenses and quietly adding Kayak affiliate links. Contributors forked it as CoMaps, immediately voted on a new name, stripped the affiliate links, and started replacing Google service dependencies. It's a clean case study in how the word 'open' can be a brand rather than a structure.

It's Not About Discs. It's About Sony Killing Ownership.
popcar.bearblog.dev

PlayStation's 2028 disc death isn't a format change — it's the completion of a decade-long project to eliminate the legal concept of owning a game: no trading, no resale, no lending to a friend, no library copy surviving a server shutdown. The essay draws a sharp line between PC going diskless (you still own the file, sort of) and console going diskless (you own a revocable license). The used-game market wasn't a bug; it was what ownership looked like.

Chat Control 1.0 Forced Through the EU's Side Door
Heise Online

After Chat Control 2.0 (mandatory suspicionless scanning of encrypted messages) stalled in the EU Parliament, the Council of Ministers used a procedural maneuver to reactivate the expired transitional regulation — the one that let tech companies 'voluntarily' scan private chats using AI and hash matching — via written procedure just before summer recess, catching Parliament off guard. The underlying legal gap opened April 3 when the old exemption from the E-Privacy Directive expired. This is the infrastructure story of the week: not a new law, but an old permission quietly reinstated.

Deep time and the senses

The Missing 500 Million Years: Asteroid Impacts Built the Continents
Ars Technica

Earth is the only planet we know of with buoyant, silica-rich continents — and geologists still don't fully agree how they formed. A new argument from Curtin University: sustained asteroid bombardment during the Hadean kept the early crust hot and thin enough that the right chemistry for buoyant continental rock could take hold, starting around 4 billion years ago. The oldest continental rocks we have are 4.03 billion years old; the half-billion years before that is almost entirely unreadable. The land you're standing on may exist because of ancient bombardment.

When the Ability to Smell Goes Away
Ars Technica

Up to 22% of the population lives with some form of smell impairment — hyposmia, anosmia, phantosmia, or parosmia (where coffee starts smelling like sewage) — yet the conditions have been chronically underdiagnosed and minimized by clinicians until COVID forced the research. The deeper thread: smell loss is emerging as an early marker for neurodegeneration, which makes the olfactory system one of the stranger frontiers in brain health. A 2023 survey found 60% of COVID patients experienced some smell loss.

Off the map

The Small Penis Rule
Wikipedia

There's a real informal legal doctrine used by authors to make roman-à-clef characters legally unchallenged: give them a small penis, and no real-world man will come forward to claim the portrait. Michael Crichton allegedly used it against journalist Michael Crowley after an unflattering review — inserting a character named 'Mick Crowley' who is a child rapist with a small penis. A Nebraska Law Review analysis argues the rule's actual power isn't legal but psychological: the humiliation cost of suing exceeds the reputational damage of the book.

For you

FILM
Leviathan
Lucien Castaing-Taylor & Véréna Paravel (Sensory Ethnography Lab, 2012)

Shot entirely on GoPros lashed to fishing nets, hooks, and the bodies of crewmen, it dissolves the boundary between camera and environment so completely that watching it feels like being inside a system rather than observing one — the ocean as infrastructure, the trawler as organism. No narration, no thesis; just the hidden mechanics of industrial harvesting rendered almost abstract and terrifying.

MUSIC
Replica
Oneohtrix Point Never

Daniel Lopatin built this record by slicing up infomercial samples into loops until they lost all meaning and became pure texture — consumer mythology collapsed into ambient drift. It sits at the exact edge where software obsession becomes emotional landscape, and the process is as interesting as the sound.

ART
Vera Molnár — retrospective works (online archive via the artist's estate)
Vera Molnár

One of the first artists to use a computer as a drawing instrument (1968), Molnár spent 50 years writing her own algorithms and then deliberately introducing errors — 'perturbations' — to study where order tips into feeling. Her grids are not cold; they are documents of a mind in conversation with a system, which is exactly the kind of obsessive narrow depth that rewards slow looking.

Happening in New York

Gothic by Design: The Dawn of Architectural Draftsmanship
The Metropolitan Museum of Art, 1000 Fifth Ave · Apr 16 – Jul 19

Last chance before it closes July 19 — an exhibition about the moment medieval builders began translating spatial imagination into drawn notation, essentially the origin story of architecture as a system of marks that describe things that don't yet exist.

Nicola Vassell Gallery: Follow Through
Nicola Vassell Gallery, 138 Tenth Ave, Chelsea · Jun 25 – Aug 7

A tight group show (Alvaro Barrington, Julia Chiang, Uman, Ming Smith, George Rouy, and others) at one of Chelsea's sharpest galleries — the kind of curated conversation across generations and mediums that rewards slower looking than a single-artist show.

Gabriel Orozco: Public Nature
JCDecaux Bus Shelters, NYC (citywide) · Jul 8 – Sep 6

Orozco's first commissioned photographic series — 10 new images installed as trompe l'oeil interventions on 300 bus shelters, designed so that a tree photographed through a concrete wall appears to extend roots into the actual sidewalk below, turning the city's infrastructure into sculptural material.

Summercon 2026
Gowanus venues (walkable cluster), Brooklyn · Jul 10–11

The long-running hacker conference transforms a cluster of Gowanus spaces into a village of talks, workshops, and parties — the rare tech gathering built around irreverence and intimacy rather than scale, where the social rituals of early internet culture are still visible before they harden.

Balloon Museum: DAYDREAM — Air Becomes Art
The Tin Building, 96 South Street, Seaport District · Jul 15 – Dec 31

The inaugural show at Balloon Museum's first permanent US flagship features site-specific commissions where air, light, and sound become architecture — including Marina Abramović's first inflatable work, Martin Creed filling a greenhouse with blue balloons, and Boris Acket's kinetic mirror piece that merges your reflection into a single mechanical wing.

Make something

Take the 445-byte world map idea and push the constraint further: pick a single country, coastline, or city grid and see how few bytes you need to make it recognizable as ASCII or SVG. Use deflate or any lossless compression you want. Then invert it — what's the minimum data that produces something that looks like a map but isn't any real place? Make a fake territory.
