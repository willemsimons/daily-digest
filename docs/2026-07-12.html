Deep time, deep space, deep sea

Why Polynesians Stopped Sailing for 1,700 Years — Then Suddenly Didn't
Ars Technica

The Lapita people reached Samoa and Tonga around 3,000 years ago, then barely moved east for 1,700 years — then between 900 and 1100 AD they exploded across the Pacific to Hawaii, New Zealand, and Easter Island within a century or so. New climate evidence is filling in what changed: the long pause wasn't timidity, it was conditions, and the sudden burst of voyaging tracks a specific climate window opening. The sweet potato evidence suggests they also made continental Americas contact — an entire chapter of human mobility hiding in a tuber.

Shackleton's Last Ship, Now a Living Reef
Ars Technica

The Quest — the wooden Norwegian whaler Shackleton died aboard in 1922, which itself sank in 1962 — has finally been imaged in full for the first time, more than 60 years on the seafloor. The wreck is more deteriorated than hoped but has become a thriving artificial reef, the ship's wooden bones now a living ecosystem. It's the same uncanny doubling as the Endurance: monument and organism simultaneously.

China Caught Its Rocket in a Net — and That's Genuinely Different
Ars Technica

CASC's Long March 10B didn't land on legs — it descended into a grid of tensioned cables stretched over an offshore ship, leaving the booster hanging in midair after engine cutoff. That makes CASC only the third organization to recover an orbital-class booster, after SpaceX and RocketLab, but using a mechanically distinct method. Reusable launch is now legitimately multi-polar, and the variant approaches are starting to diverge.

A Disco Ball Measured How Earth Drags Spacetime — to 0.2% Precision
Ars Technica

LARES-2 is a 295-kg solid sphere of nickel-chromium alloy covered in 303 retroreflectors, with no thrusters, no panels, no electronics — just mass and reflectivity, giving it the lowest area-to-mass ratio of any satellite ever launched. By bouncing lasers off it continuously, researchers measured Earth's Lense-Thirring frame-dragging (the way a rotating mass pulls spacetime with it) to 0.2% uncertainty, down from several percent. Einstein proposed this effect; it took a glorified mirror ball to actually nail it.

Mind, culture, making

2.6 Billion Sketches Reveal What Language Hides About How We Think
arXiv

Researchers analyzed 2.6 billion Quick Draw sketches from 236 countries to ask whether visual imagination reveals conceptual structure that language compresses away — and it does, substantially. Visual representations align 45% more closely with established cultural distances than text-based measures do, and the variation is strongest for concepts involving touch and physical interaction, suggesting that visual imagery encodes embodied experience rather than shared convention. Language, in other words, is a lossy codec; sketching is closer to the raw file.

Octopus Intelligence Breaks the Theory That Sociality Builds Big Brains
Nautilus

The dominant framework — the social brain hypothesis — says large brains co-evolved with the demands of navigating complex group dynamics, which is why primate brain size correlates with social group size. Octopuses are solitary, territorial, cannibalistic, and don't parent their young — and yet they're extraordinarily intelligent, use tools, and play. A new paper in iScience proposes the asocial learning pathway as a parallel route to intelligence, which doesn't just complicate the theory; it suggests that brains solving hard environmental problems alone can generate the same cognitive complexity as brains solving hard social problems together.

The Funguy Project: Using Lasers to Print with Living Fungi
Hackaday

Kexin Wang trained a temporal convolutional neural network on time-lapse growth images of three fungal species, coupled it with a cellular automaton where each cell runs a small neural network learning local growth rules, and used that model to direct a 405nm laser diode — exploiting fungi's photophobia to steer growth into arbitrary shapes on agar. The model also predicts nutrient depletion zones so the laser skips areas where fungi won't spread anyway. It's a generative system where the substrate is alive and the 'renderer' is a biological organism responding to light.

Terry Tao Let an AI Agent Port Two Dozen Applets — Here's the Honest Account
Terry Tao's Blog

Tao has been building math visualization applets since 1999 in Java 1.0 — including a honeycomb applet he wrote with Allen Knutson that became inaccessible when browsers dropped Java support. In a few hours, a coding agent ported roughly two dozen of them to JavaScript, found two bugs in Tao's original code he didn't know were there, introduced only one minor new bug, and added colorization to the Besicovitch set applet as an unrequested upgrade. The reliability rate from someone this exacting is itself the data point.

Off the map

Leonardo da Vinci Might Have Slowed Technology Down
Sentiers

Tim O'Reilly, building on Jeff Ding's framework, argues that technological progress is driven by the accumulation of ordinary engineering applications rather than heroic individual invention — and the Sentiers piece takes this further with the provocative corollary: the genius myth may actively retard progress by concentrating attribution and attention in ways that obscure the distributed, incremental systems doing the actual work. The case against Leonardo isn't that he wasn't brilliant; it's that the narrative of his brilliance is bad epistemology that we keep repeating.

Carax builds an entire film around the texture of a scene — youth, longing, and doomed systems coexisting in a Paris that feels like a frontier civilization right before it hardens. ART
UUmwelt
Pierre Huyghe

A machine-learning system reconstructs mental images from recorded brain activity, then responds in real time to the gazes of visitors — the screens change based on who's looking. It's not 'AI art' as spectacle; it's a live ecosystem of two kinds of intelligence co-producing imagination, and one of the sharpest current examples of art as an open-ended, self-regulating system rather than an object.
