How tools think (and how we think about tools)

Good Tools Are Invisible
Gingerbill

A clean, sharp essay making the case that the correct criterion for a tool is invisibility — flow state, not puzzle-solving. The specific target is the vim-as-identity phenomenon: treating a tool's friction as the point, marketing limitations as features. The author's test is blunt: if someone can do the same task in Sublime with multiple cursors in a minute while you're still building a macro, the macro isn't the win you think it is. Useful provocation for anyone who makes things that other people use.

Clay Extruder Enables Printable Pottery
Hackaday

Joshua Bird built a ceramic 3D printer from scratch, solving the clay rheology problem (soft enough to flow, stiff enough to bridge) by pressurizing the clay feed as much as possible — which lets you use stiffer mixtures. The killer detail: every plastic auger he 3D-printed was abraded to nothing by the clay in under an hour; only a stainless steel printed auger survived. The result is a working chain-mail mesh fired in ceramic, which is a sentence that shouldn't exist.

Off the map

Gil Batle's double life encoded in porcelain
Colossal

Gil Batle spent 25 years incarcerated in California prisons, teaching himself to draw and eventually tattooing clandestinely. Now living in the Philippines, he encodes those memories into white porcelain plates alongside Hiligaynon folk flora — bird cages, chains, shivs, and barbs nested inside the most genteel domestic object imaginable. The structural tension is the whole point: the fragility and implied civility of porcelain holding scenes of brutality. Show at Ricco/Maresca in New York through August 21.

For you

FILM
Tetsuo: The Iron Man
Shinya Tsukamoto

A film that is essentially a WebGL shader made with human bodies — industrial noise, cybernetic body horror, and feedback loops rendered in frantic 16mm. It treats the boundary between flesh and machine as something that collapses rather than a line to cross, which should speak directly to someone working at the physical/digital edge.

ART
Coded Environments (ongoing web works)
Casey Reas

Reas writes software as a drawing practice — his process-based pieces generate forms that feel both organic and rigidly governed by rule systems, making the code itself the brushstroke. The work sits at exactly the intersection of generative 3D aesthetics and systems-thinking that Willem builds in.

MUSIC
Music Has the Right to Children
Boards of Canada

The record functions like a found-footage reel of a childhood that never happened — built from degraded tape, field recordings, and half-remembered melodies that feel like memory itself decaying. Its emotional density comes entirely from texture and structure, with no lyrics to explain itself, which is the music equivalent of generative work that lands in the gut.
