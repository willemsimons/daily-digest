Signal from space and from deep time

First confirmed atmosphere on a rocky, habitable-zone exoplanet
BBC Science

JWST spectroscopy detected helium in the atmosphere of LHS 1140 b — a rocky planet 48 light-years away orbiting in the Goldilocks zone of a red dwarf. Helium alone doesn't support life, but its presence means the planet has held onto *something* against stellar radiation long enough to matter, and other gases may follow in subsequent observations. Of 6,000+ known exoplanets, this is the first time anyone has confirmed an atmosphere on a rocky world in a habitable zone.

The computer at the bottom of a canal — and why it was right about everything
Negroni Venture Studios

In 1988, Linn Products — the Glasgow hi-fi company that made the Sondek LP12 turntable — built custom silicon that checked memory bounds in hardware, garbage-collected its own heap in silicon, and treated memory and disk as a single persistent object store. The chip ended up in the Forth and Clyde Canal; the ideas it embodied are now shipping in production ARM silicon in 2026. A perfect object-with-a-life essay about being right in the wrong decade.

Making and seeing

Regressive JPEGs: iterative compression as a generative medium
maurycyz.com

Progressive JPEG stores image data in multiple scan passes — DC coefficients first, then successively finer frequency bins — and this project runs that process in reverse and iteratively, using each compression artifact as the input to the next pass. The result is a controlled descent into Fourier-space hallucination, where the image structure slowly dissolves into its own low-frequency ghost. It's compression as erosion, and the outputs look like nothing else.

Cross-sectioning crickets inside a scanning electron microscope with a femtosecond laser
Hackaday

Femtosecond lasers ablate material without heating it — the pulses are so short the energy has no time to conduct — which lets Ben Krasnow cut clean cross-sections of chemically fixed, solvent-exchanged, argon-charred crickets while they sit inside a live SEM. One unexpected problem: the paint on his shutter was getting coated with charged carbon, forming a capacitor that flooded the electron image with noise. The resulting insect cross-sections are stunning and deeply strange.

Off the map

Vāgdhenu: a TTS engine built for Vedic Sanskrit chanting, meter and all
prathosh.in

Standard TTS destroys Sanskrit chanting because it ignores svara (tonal accent) and vṛtta (meter) — the prosodic rules that distinguish a recitation from a reading. This system, built at IISc Bengaluru, auto-detects meter from any Indian script input and renders a meter-aware chanted output, trained on a purpose-recorded single-speaker corpus with a fine-tuned neural vocoder. It now powers the complete 12-skandha Śrīmad Bhāgavatam app with synced karaoke highlighting — software as oral tradition preservation.

For you

FILM
Sans Soleil
Chris Marker

A film that operates as a meditation on memory, ritual, and how images construct reality — Marker drifts between Tokyo, Guinea-Bissau, and Iceland, building a theory of time and forgetting through essayistic montage. Structurally strange, obsessively crafted, impossible to shake. Exactly the kind of deep-systems-meets-expressive-surface work that earns repeat viewings.

ART
UUmwelt
Pierre Huyghe

Machine learning trained on recorded brain activity reconstructs imagined images in real time on freestanding screens in MoMA's Sculpture Garden — and the gazes of visitors actively alter the output. It's a living feedback loop between human cognition and nonhuman intelligence, a system that runs on attention itself. Huyghe's framing — 'a collective production of imagination between two kinds of intelligences' — is the kind of proposition that makes you want to go make something.

Make something

Take a single image — a photo you took, anything — and write a script that saves it as a progressive JPEG, then re-loads and re-saves it 30–50 times in a loop, each time reducing quality by one notch. Export every 5th iteration as a frame and assemble them into a short video or flipbook. You're not degrading the image; you're watching it fall back through its own frequency decomposition. Pay attention to which structures survive longest.
