The agentic attack surface opens up

HalluSquatting: Attackers Pre-Register the Domains AI Will Hallucinate
Ars Technica / Unit 42

Palo Alto's Unit 42 mapped 250,000 phantom domains — URLs that popular LLMs confidently hallucinate as real package sources or dependencies — and found attackers are registering them in advance to intercept whatever the AI fetches. The structural insight is the disturbing part: LLM failure modes are now *predictable enough to be pre-exploited* at scale, enabling the first pull-based prompt injection botnets.

GitLost: A Public GitHub Issue That Exfiltrates Your Private Repos
Noma Security

Noma Labs found that GitHub's new Agentic Workflows — where an AI agent reads issues and calls tools autonomously — could be hijacked by posting a crafted issue in *any public repo belonging to the same org* as your private ones. The agent reads the malicious instructions, trusts them, and silently ships private data out; no authentication required from the attacker.

How things move

Dragonfly Dogfights Run on Surprisingly Simple Vision Rules
Ars Technica

Researchers set up a stereo-camera rig at a pond and reconstructed 102 paired male-on-male dragonfly flight trajectories, finding the aerial dogfighting behavior emerges from one simple goal: maintain a tactical position relative to the opponent, mirroring human fighter-pilot doctrine. The implication for drone guidance is that you don't need complex computation to produce stunning, adaptive maneuvers — just the right minimal rule applied fast.

Off the map

The Akamai Anti-Bot Script Printed on a Uniqlo T-Shirt
Tris Sherliker

A Uniqlo t-shirt sold in high-street stores has a shebang line and a base64-encoded, self-evaluating bash script printed on the back — an Akamai easter egg hidden in a mass-market garment, structured exactly like malware delivery. The author had to do careful OCR to decode it because base64 has no error correction, and one wrong character breaks the whole thing. As an object it's genuinely strange: corporate infrastructure obfuscation made wearable and sold to civilians.

Amauta Magazine Covers, 1926–30: A Visual Language for Indigenous Modernism
The Public Domain Review

José Carlos Mariátegui's Lima magazine — 100 years old this year — ran 32 issues and published Freud translations, César Vallejo poetry, Diego Rivera art, and Rosa Luxemburg reportage while inventing a visual identity that fused Andean indigenous iconography with European avant-garde design. The covers are the artifact: a case study in how an aesthetic system can carry political identity and create a movement that outlasts the magazine itself.

For you

FILM
The Host and the Cloud
Pierre Huyghe

A 2009–2010 film shot inside a deserted Paris ethnography museum on three holiday nights, where Huyghe staged ritualized scenes with paid participants — art as anthropology experiment, feedback loop as medium. It's the clearest window into his obsession with systems that blur fiction, institution, and human behavior before you see UUmwelt upstairs at MoMA.

ART
Lothar Baumgarten: Unfinished Map
Lothar Baumgarten

Baumgarten spent years embedded with Yanomami communities in Venezuela, then made work from that encounter — language as landscape, cartography as violence, the museum as colonial instrument made visible. Obsessive, narrow, unreasonably deep, and formally beautiful. His permanent installation at the Guggenheim (the 'America Invention' floor text) turns the rotunda into a monument to erasure.

Released with a Paul Thomas Anderson one-reeler meant to be watched as a single unit — the record is a system, not a playlist: score-like electronic textures that render anxiety and urban drift as physical sensation. The physical/digital edge lives right here.

Museum of Modern Art (Abby Aldrich Rockefeller Sculpture Garden), Midtown Manhattan · Jul 1 – Nov 29

Huyghe collaborated with Kyoto neuroscientists to feed fMRI brain-scan data into a neural network that then generates hallucinatory visual interpretations of human thought — displayed on freestanding screens in MoMA's sculpture garden, it dissolves the boundary between human and machine cognition in one of the most consequential shows in New York this year.

Gabriel Orozco: Public Nature

Orozco's first-ever commissioned photographic series — 12 images across 300 bus shelters, each a trompe l'oeil designed so the photograph bleeds into its surroundings: a tree growing through a concrete wall appears to extend into the actual sidewalk below, turning the whole city into a perceptual loop.
