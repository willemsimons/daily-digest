Biology of attention

Your Brain Briefly Listens to Two Conversations at Once — and EEG Can See It
PLOS Biology

When you shift attention between two simultaneous speakers, your auditory cortex doesn't simply switch — it runs both streams in parallel for a measurable overlap window, engaging the new target before releasing the old one. The EEG signature of this moment is a drop in alpha power, a known index of cognitive load, and the researchers used LLMs to model four different lexical-prediction strategies to figure out how the brain resets its language context mid-switch. The implication: selective attention is less like a toggle and more like a brief chord before the hand moves to a new key.

Some People Are Better Eavesdroppers — What's Different in Their Brains
Nautilus

The readable companion to the PLOS Biology paper above: individual differences in this dual-stream encoding ability are measurable, consistent, and reflect something structural about how people's auditory cortices handle competing signals. The study framing — participants wore EEG harnesses listening to two simultaneous TED Talks amid background noise — is elegant in its mundanity. Worth reading back-to-back with the primary paper; together they make a clean argument for attention as a spectrum rather than a switch.

Making and unmaking representation

Microsoft Comic Chat Is Open Source — and It's Weirder Than You Remember
Microsoft Open Source Blog

The 1996 IRC client that turned live text conversations into auto-generated comic panels — choosing poses, expressions, and panel layouts in real time via an expert system — is now fully on GitHub. It's also, incidentally, the software that gave Comic Sans its first real home: Vincent Connare designed the font specifically because speech bubbles needed something that read as spoken rather than typed. What's genuinely interesting isn't the nostalgia but the underlying design question it was answering: how do you add emotional valence and non-verbal signal to text-only communication without video? That question is still open.

Decoy Font: A Typeface That Reads One Thing to Humans, Another to AI
Mixfont Experiments

Built on spatial frequency separation — thin high-frequency outlines carry the decoy text that AI reads when analyzing pixels up close, while a blurred low-frequency mass spells the real message, legible when you step back or squint — the font exploits the fact that most LLM vision pipelines process images at close zoom. It's a TTF you can actually install and type with, and it reliably fools GPT and Gemini. Less a privacy tool than a working proof that the gap between human and machine perception is still wide enough to drive a typeface through.

Off the map

A 1,900-Year-Old Roman Latrine Reveals Concrete That Heals Its Own Cracks
Smithsonian Magazine

Researchers went to Hadrian's Villa specifically to study the communal toilets — unrestored, untouched, analytically pristine — and found that Roman concrete's millennium-scale durability isn't just pozzolanic chemistry (volcanic ash + lime + water) but also carbonation: CO₂ diffusing into cracks reacts with calcium to form calcite, physically sealing the damage over decades. Modern concrete typically fails within a century; Roman infrastructure is still load-bearing after two thousand years, and the latrine turns out to be the cleanest laboratory for understanding why. Published in Science Advances July 8.

Make something

Build a small spatial-frequency compositor: take any short sentence, render it twice — once as sharp high-frequency outlines (the decoy), once as a soft low-frequency blur (the real message) — and overlay them into a single image. Test whether a human reading at arm's length sees the blurred layer while a screenshot fed to an LLM reads the outline layer. You don't need the font; you can fake the effect in a canvas shader or even Photoshop blend modes. The interesting question is how far you can push the legibility gap before both layers collapse into noise.
