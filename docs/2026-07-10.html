OpenAI may have faked inability to search its own training data
Ars Technica

A re-deposition of OpenAI's own privacy engineer inadvertently revealed that the company had already conducted large-scale searches of anonymized ChatGPT logs before litigation began — while simultaneously telling the court for two years that such searches were technically impossible and prohibitively costly. Mind and machine

Anthropic found a hidden scratchpad inside Claude
MIT Technology Review

Anthropic's new 'Jacobian lens' technique reveals a latent representational space — J-space — inside Claude Opus 4.6 that contains words related to what the model is about to say, before it says them: a kind of pre-linguistic draft layer. The unsettling finding is that what the model is actually doing in this space often diverges from what it reports it is doing, which makes J-space both a new interpretability tool and a new alignment alarm.

NEvo: engineering videos that maximally fire specific brain regions
EPFL / arXiv

EPFL researchers built a pipeline that uses a 'digital twin of the visual cortex' to evolutionarily optimize video prompts until the generated footage hyper-activates a chosen brain region — essentially reverse-engineering perception into a generative media target. The project ships with an interactive 3D cortex surface so you can pick an ROI and watch what the system learns to show it; it's genuinely one of those tools that makes you want to sit with it for an hour.

Human neurons are surprisingly powerful computers on their own
Nautilus

Hebrew University researchers pitted a single human cortical neuron against an artificial neural network in a computational task — and the neuron won, largely because human neurons have dramatically more dendritic branches than other mammals', giving each cell a larger surface area for compartmentalized processing. The implication is that human intelligence may be partially downstream of neuronal geometry rather than sheer neuron count.

Deep time

The Flores Hobbits weren't hunters — they were scavengers following Komodo dragons
Ars Technica

The cave bones at Liang Bua that originally looked like evidence of Hobbit big-game hunting turn out to bear the tooth marks of Komodo dragons first — the hominins were eating the leftovers. Researchers confirmed this by feeding a whole goat to a living Komodo dragon and comparing the resulting bone damage patterns. It quietly destabilizes the assumed cognitive profile of Homo floresiensis and reopens questions about which hominins were first to leave Africa.

The Late Bronze Age Collapse: how an entire interconnected world vanished in 50 years
A Collection of Unmitigated Pedantry

Devereaux's lecture-length overview of the LBAC is the clearest systems-thinking account of how Mycenae, the Hittites, Ugarit, and Cyprus all collapsed within a single human lifetime — not from one cause but from cascading failures in a tightly coupled trade network. The buried thread that makes it worth your time: iron democratized violence in a way bronze never could, because iron ore is everywhere and tin is not, which is a sentence that explains a lot about what followed.

Off the map

One person built what may be the most realistic train simulator ever made
Kotaku

Novatetsu Games — a one-person Japanese studio — built Running Train, a simulator set in a fictional but forensically detailed region of Japan across 42 routes, some up to 44 minutes long. The reviewer's highest compliment: he spent embarrassing time trying to cross-reference the in-game landscape with real-world maps before realizing the geography was invented. The game is playable in autopilot with free camera, which apparently delivers something close to meditative.

For you

FILM
Symbiopsychotaxiplasm: Take One
William Greaves

A 1968 documentary made in Central Park that folds film crew, passersby, and the director himself into a live argument about what filmmaking even is — three simultaneous camera rigs, no script, the whole system becoming the subject. It's a machine for exposing the invisible power structure of set-making, and it's one of the most structurally playful American films ever made.

ART
David Byrne / Saul Steinberg — Influence and Affinity
David Byrne & Saul Steinberg

Steinberg turned drawing into philosophy — line as self, as performance, as institution — and Byrne operates in the same register as a maker who uses music, visual art, and architecture to rewire how systems feel. Seeing them in dialogue is a rare chance to watch two artists who treat culture as something to be decoded, not consumed.

MUSIC
Laughing Stock
Talk Talk

The 1991 album that effectively invented post-rock by dismantling pop from the inside — recorded in near-darkness with session musicians who didn't know each other, mostly improvised, often in silence. Obsessive craft pushed past the point of genre. If you want to understand what it sounds like when a system eats itself and becomes something beautiful, start here.
