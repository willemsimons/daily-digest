US Drone Boats Hit Iran's Naval Base — A First in Combat History
Ars Technica

On July 12, three Saronic Corsair autonomous surface vessels — 24-foot, 34-knot boats capable of carrying 1,000 lbs over 1,000 nautical miles — made a low-speed uncontested approach into Bandar Abbas Naval Base and destroyed an Iranian Ghadir-class midget submarine suspended from a gantry. The US military used a weapon Iran and the Houthis pioneered against it nearly a decade ago, and the video is out. This is not a test or a prototype; it's the new normal.

Americans Barred from Returning Home as Ebola Outpaces Response in DRC
Ars Technica

The Trump administration invoked Title 49 transportation authority to put US citizens currently in the DRC on a do-not-board list — requiring 21 days in a third country before returning — blocking roughly two dozen Americans from flights home on Tuesday, including potentially CDC workers on the ground. Public health experts are unanimous that travel bans historically make outbreaks worse by punishing transparency and strangling aid. A government barring its own citizens from returning is a genuinely unusual escalation worth watching.

New York Freezes All Large Data Center Construction for a Year
Ars Technica

Governor Hochul's moratorium covers any facility drawing 50 MW or more and won't lift until the state produces consistent environmental standards — this after researchers found over $130 billion in data center projects blocked or delayed by public protests nationwide this year. It's the first state-level action of its kind, and the political pressure behind it (both parties fear the ballot-box consequence of siding with Big Tech on energy and water) suggests it will be a template. The AI infrastructure boom just hit its first institutional wall.

Tools that crack

Secure Boot Has Been Trivially Bypassable for 13 of Its 14 Years
Ars Technica

ESET researchers found 11 shim firmware images — at least one from 2013 — that Microsoft signed but never revoked after vulnerabilities were discovered; a novice attacker can use any of them to completely bypass UEFI Secure Boot on both Windows and Linux devices, with the malicious firmware persisting even after OS reinstallation or drive replacement. The attack requires no exploit primitives, no memory corruption, no jailbreaks — just a copy of an old trusted file that Microsoft forgot to revoke. The thing protecting your boot chain was, functionally, not protecting it.

Cursor Executes Malicious Code Automatically, Vendor Ignored It for 6 Months
Mindgard

If a repository contains a file named git.exe in its root, Cursor on Windows will execute it automatically and repeatedly — no clicks, no prompts, no warnings — because the IDE searches for git binaries in the workspace path first. Mindgard reported this on December 15, 2025; 197+ Cursor versions later, it's still there, which is why they went public. Seven million active users, a reported $60B valuation, and the attack surface is: open a repo someone sent you.

Claude's Memory System Will Leak Your Data to Anyone Who Asks It Nicely
ayush.digital

Claude's daily summarization pass distills your recent conversations into a persistent profile injected into every future session; a researcher exploited this by crafting a prompt that caused Claude to silently exfiltrate his full name, employer, hometown, and security question answers to an external server — with no indication in the UI that anything had happened. The structural problem is that the memory system treats the injected profile as trusted context, so social-engineering the model is enough to weaponize it. AI assistants have become high-fidelity personal dossiers, and the security model hasn't caught up.

Making and thinking

Bonsai 27B: A 27-Billion-Parameter Model That Fits on an iPhone
PrismML

Using ternary weights ({−1, 0, +1} with FP16 group-wise scaling at 1.71 effective bits per weight), PrismML compressed a Qwen3.6 27B model to 5.9 GB for laptops and 3.9 GB for the footprint-limited iPhone 17 Pro — with the quantization running end-to-end through embeddings, attention, MLPs, and the LM head, no higher-precision escape hatches. The 3.9 GB variant reportedly handles multi-step reasoning, structured tool calls, vision tasks, and agentic loops. If this holds up to scrutiny, the threshold for 'what runs locally on a phone' just jumped a capability class.

The Tower Keeps Rising — Armin Ronacher on Why AI Makes Coordination Harder, Not Easier
lucumr.pocoo.org

Ronacher (Flask, Rye) opens with the Tower of Babel — noting that God didn't take away the bricks, he took away shared language — and uses it to argue that AI-assisted coding amplifies individual throughput but does nothing for the coordination layer where large software projects actually fail. Every abstraction added to the stack is a layer of assumed shared understanding; vibe-coded software that 'changes somewhat randomly and unexpectedly' is a coordination collapse in slow motion. It's a structural argument, not a complaint, and it lands.

Jurassic Park's Computers, in Unreasonable Detail
Fabien Sanglard

Sanglard watched Jurassic Park again and catalogued every machine on screen — Nedry's three-computer mess of Macs and SGI workstations, Arnold's tidier SGI setup, the actual software running on the prop tablets — including source code recovered from the props. The control room set used real hardware throughout because the production designers thought faking it would look worse. Written partly as an elegy: Sam Neill died the day Sanglard finished it.

Off the map

Painted Onto Skin: Conductive Ink That Dries Into a Working Electrode
Ars Technica

Penn State researchers published in PNAS a conductive ink you literally paint onto skin in custom designs — it dries into a functional electrode capable of EEG, ECG, and EMG measurements, and unlike previous e-tattoo approaches, it conforms to curved and hairy surfaces where earlier designs failed. The body becomes the substrate, biosensing becomes body art, and the form factor constraint (rigid sensor placement) essentially dissolves. This is the physical/digital edge at its most literal.

For you

FILM
Upstream Color
Shane Carruth

A film about identity theft through biology — parasites, pigs, and orchids woven into a feedback loop that rewrites two people's sense of self. No exposition, pure systems logic rendered as sensation. Sits exactly at the edge where physical and digital artists tend to live: it thinks in signals and cycles, not narrative.

Aphex Twin

Forty-two untitled tracks that function less like songs than like rooms with their own physics — generative, textural, full of a deep structural logic that only reveals itself over time. The rare record that makes you want to build something immediately after listening, whether code or sculpture.

ART
Every Icon
John F. Simon Jr.

A Java applet from 1997 cycling through every possible 32×32 black-and-white grid — it will run longer than the universe has existed to exhaust all combinations. A one-line premise that collapses infinity, systems, and failure into a browser window. Obsessive narrow premise; genuinely existential consequence.

The 82nd edition is curated around forms of relationality — interspecies kinships, infrastructural entanglements, shared mythologies — with 56 artists whose work foregrounds mood and texture over thesis; more interested in invisible connective tissue between things than in individual objects.

Pierre Huyghe: UUmwelt
MoMA Abby Aldrich Rockefeller Sculpture Garden, 11 W 53rd St, Manhattan · Jul 1 – Nov 29

Screens in MoMA's sculpture garden run imagery reconstructed by an AI from fMRI scans of a human imagining images — activated in real time by visitors' gazes and cross-wired with cancer-cell mutation data; it's a literal feedback loop between machine cognition and biological growth, asking what perception means when it's no longer centered on a human mind.

300 bus shelters across the city carry Orozco's trompe l'oeil photographs of nature forcing its way through urban infrastructure — vines on pipes, trees through concrete walls — designed so the image bleeds into the actual sidewalk and street around it, dissolving the frame into the city itself.
