World

A Hacker Erased Romania's Entire Land Registry
Risky Biz

After a failed extortion attempt, a single attacker entered Romania's cadastre agency using valid credentials, mapped the systems, then wiped both the databases and all backups — leaving the country's entire real-estate market legally paralyzed for over a week. Notaries can't record transactions; citizens can't prove they own their homes. The incident reframes the threat model for civil infrastructure: it's not about theft anymore, it's about erasure of the evidentiary layer that property law runs on.

China's Free AI Models Are Splitting the White House
MIT Technology Review

Moonshot's Kimi K3 — free, open-weights, and reportedly competitive with GPT-class models — has fractured Trump's AI advisors into open-source advocates and closed-model protectionists, with David Sacks calling Anthropic's output "lobotomized" and Pentagon officials trading insults with OpenAI publicly. The core problem is structural: every smart free Chinese model erodes the revenue case for US frontier labs, whose valuations are propping up a meaningful slice of current economic optimism. China isn't just competing on capability — it's competing on price point, and zero is a hard price to beat.

The AI inflection that's harder to spin

AI Is Now Generating Counterexamples to Unsolved Math Problems Faster Than Humans Can Follow
Xena Project

In May 2026, ChatGPT disproved Erdős' Unit Distance conjecture — a decades-old open problem in discrete geometry — by connecting it to a 1960s theorem in number theory. Within a week, a system co-founded by Yann LeCun's company had auto-formalized the entire proof in Lean, the machine-checkable proof language, and got it verified. The author of this post is a formal verification mathematician who spent nine years arguing humans couldn't be trusted with technical proofs; his measured alarm about what's happening right now is worth reading carefully.

Reverse-Engineering Anything at Home Is Now Basically Free
Simon Willison's Weblog

Willison's observation is deceptively simple: coding agents didn't make reverse-engineering possible — it was always possible — they just collapsed the ROI calculus entirely. The psychological weight of "is this worth it if the API breaks next month" dissolves when rewriting is nearly free. For hardware tinkerers and makers, this is a genuine phase shift: devices that were practically closed are now practically open.

Who Actually Benefits From Banning Chinese Models?
Stratechery

Ben Thompson cuts straight to the incentive hypocrisy: the same labs lobbying to ban Chinese open-weights models for "distillation" trained their own models on unlicensed data at scale. His deeper point is an Aggregation Theory inversion — zero marginal cost software once favored demand aggregators, but AI's compute costs bring old industrial economics back, and open-weights Chinese models are the wrench in that machinery. The national security framing is real but also conveniently useful for incumbents with a revenue problem.

Vincent Woo

Shinjuku Station Modeled in Three.js
Hacker News

Someone took official Japanese government indoor mapping data for Shinjuku — the world's busiest station, notorious for being a near-impossible labyrinth with 200 exits — and rendered it in Three.js as a navigable 3D space. The subject is perfect for this treatment: a building that is already experienced as a non-Euclidean puzzle, now made literal.

Jelly UI: Soft-Body Physics for Ordinary HTML Form Controls
Hacker News

A dependency-free Web Components library that gives standard form elements — buttons, inputs, toggles — real soft-body physics: they squish, jiggle, and settle. It ships with WCAG AA color tokens and dark mode support, so it's not a toy. The gap between what you expect an interface to feel like and what this one does is exactly where the delight lives.

Off the map

Ex Situ: A Map That Draws Arcs Between Where Objects Were Stolen and Where They Sit Now
Hacker News

Ex Situ is an open-source spatial index of displaced cultural artifacts — it maps looted or removed objects from their origin points to the Western museums that currently hold them, visualized as arcs across a global map. It turns the repatriation debate into a cartographic argument: geography as moral ledger. The format makes the scale of displacement legible in a way that lists and legal briefs never quite do.
