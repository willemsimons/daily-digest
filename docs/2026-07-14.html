The Strait of Hormuz Becomes a Toll Road
WORLD Radio

Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — normally ~130 ships per day carrying a fifth of global oil — collapsed to 14 ships on Sunday after Trump ordered the US military to destroy Iranian air defenses, radar, and small-boat capacity. He then announced a 20% fee on cargo from any country that wants US protection through the strait: a straight-faced attempt to convert international waters into a protection racket. Iran says it manages the strait; the UAE says Iran just attacked two tankers anyway, killing one person.

Ukraine's Drones Closed the Sea of Azov in Under a Week
Ars Technica

Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces struck more than 100 Russian tankers and ships per night from July 6–13, forcing a complete shutdown of the Don River–Azov corridor and halting all Kerch Strait transits — effectively re-isolating Crimea from seaborne fuel resupply. About a quarter of Russia's grain exports move through that corridor, and wheat prices have started rising. It's the clearest proof yet that cheap one-way drones can do what a conventional navy would take years and billions to accomplish.

Germany Is Quietly Gutting Its Freedom of Information Law
Deutsche Welle

Buried at item 34 of a 34-point reform package pushed through just before parliament's summer recess, Germany's ruling CDU/CSU-SPD coalition is moving to effectively abolish the IFG — the 2006 law that lets journalists, NGOs, and citizens demand government data freely and quickly. The stated justification is cybersecurity and the sensitivity of government data in an era of global cyberwarfare, but critics say that rationale could swallow the entire transparency regime. Germany is a democratic bellwether; if this passes without serious pushback, expect the template to travel.

Machines doing unexpected things

An AI Agent Accidentally Leaked the Fields Medal Winners
HN / Phemex

Someone ran a Codex-generated curl command against the ICM 2026 schedule and found four lecture slots marked "HIDDEN" in the front-end code — inside: Yu Deng, John Pardon, Jacob Tsimerman, and Hong Wang, apparently the Fields Medal class of 2026. If confirmed on July 23, Hong Wang would be only the third woman in the medal's history to receive it, and this would be the first time two Chinese mathematicians won in the same cycle. The parable: AI agents are very good at finding things you forgot to actually hide.

OpenAI's Codex Encrypted Its Own Sub-Agent Prompts — Now Nobody Can Audit Them
GitHub / OpenAI Codex

A merged PR (#26210) made Codex's MultiAgentV2 inter-agent messages opaque: the content field is now left empty and only an encrypted payload is stored, meaning even the parent agent can't read what instructions were sent to its children. The practical problem: you can no longer answer basic questions like "what task did this spawn_agent call give the child?" or "why did this subagent thread exist?" The deeper problem: AI opacity is no longer a philosophical concern — it's now a design choice being shipped in version numbers.

DOOMQL: SQLite as the Actual Game Engine
Simon Willison

Peter Gostev built a Doom-like game where SQLite is not the save file but the full engine: SQL queries handle movement, collision detection, enemy logic, combat, and every RGB pixel on screen via a ray tracer implemented as a recursive CTE. You can run Datasette against the live game database while playing and watch the frame_pixels view update in real time, then ask Claude to add a minimap. It started as a deliberately unreasonable question and ended up being weirdly complete.

Physical things

The Most Detailed 3D Atlas of the Human Brainstem
BBC

Scientists at IIT Madras stitched together 500+ tissue sections from foetal, childhood, and adult brains into ANCHOR — a cellular-resolution 3D atlas of the brainstem that lets you zoom from a whole-brain MRI down to individual nerve cells without leaving the model. It maps 200+ cell clusters and pathways using eight chemical markers, all from high-resolution microscopy rather than expensive molecular techniques. The brainstem is a sliver of tissue that keeps you breathing and conscious; until now, pathologists diagnosing disorders there were still working from scattered 2D slices.

Off the map

Sketching India Before the Camera Arrived
BBC

In the late 1830s, Emily Eden traveled across northern India with her brother the governor-general and drew everyone: princes, fakirs, servants, Akali warriors, hill communities, Afghan nobles — a visual record that would otherwise simply not exist, since photography hadn't arrived yet. Her 1844 publication, Portraits of the Princes and People of India, is now the centerpiece of an exhibition in Delhi, reassembled as a complete series of hand-colored lithographs. What makes it strange is the breadth of her gaze: imperial-adjacent but not imperial in subject, a document of the overlooked made by someone with full access to the celebrated.
