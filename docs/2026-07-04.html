The craft of making software

Wordgard 0.1 — Marijn Haverbeke starts over, again
marijnhaverbeke.nl

The person who built ProseMirror and CodeMirror — the substrate under almost every serious browser editor in existence — has shipped his sixth non-trivial editor, not as a patch on what came before but as a clean break with a new name. He's candid that ProseMirror has parts that make him wince, and that grafting fixes onto it backward-compatibly would produce something 'win32-style.' What's interesting here isn't the software but the posture: someone at the top of a niche choosing to restart rather than calcify.

Hunting a 16-year-old SQLite bug with TLA+
Ubuntu / Canonical

A write-ahead log bug sat undetected in SQLite — one of the most audited pieces of software on earth — from 2010 until 2026, then was finally cornered using TLA+ formal verification as a detective tool rather than a proof tool. The Canonical team used it to model the exact sequence of states that produce database corruption, then built a second model of how their own dqlite layer uses SQLite to check whether the corruption path is reachable from above. It's a case study in how formal methods become useful not for guaranteeing correctness but for making failure legible.

Bodies, cells, and the pipeline

Making old blood stem cells young again — the lysosome angle
Cell Stem Cell / Mount Sinai

Mount Sinai researchers identified overactive, damaged lysosomes — the cell's internal recycling centers — as a primary driver of blood stem cell aging in mice, not a downstream symptom but a causal mechanism. By calming lysosomal overactivity, they restored the stem cells' ability to regenerate balanced blood and immune cells at youthful levels. This is early-stage mouse work, but the mechanistic specificity is what makes it notable: it points to a defined intervention target rather than a vague 'aging pathway.'

Elecoglipron — the oral GLP-1 pill that actually worked in Phase 2b
Mass General Brigham / The Lancet

The SOLSTICE trial, published simultaneously in The Lancet and presented at ADA Scientific Sessions, showed that elecoglipron — an oral GLP-1 receptor agonist — significantly lowered blood glucose and reduced body weight in people with type 2 diabetes. Oral delivery is the key unlock: the injectable GLP-1 market is massive, but needle-aversion and cold-chain logistics lock out a large share of potential patients. A pill that delivers comparable results changes the access geometry of the entire drug class.

Making as politics, resistance as aesthetic

Summer of Ludd — Gen Z's anti-tech festival is happening in Tompkins Square Park right now
Ars Technica

A weeklong festival in the East Village — papier-mâché puppets, a small orchestra in Pride regalia, zines on getting off Spotify and surveillance in schools, workshops on flirting offline — is staging the actual Luddite history (artisans resisting industrial displacement, met with state violence) as a live organizing frame for 2026. The interesting part isn't the phone-free posturing; it's that the original Luddites weren't anti-technology, they were anti-the-specific-use-of-technology-to-destroy-their-livelihoods, and this crowd seems to know that. A scene forming in real time, a few blocks from you.

Off the map

The world's tallest tropical trees have no hydraulic ceiling — the assumption was wrong
University of Exeter / Science

For decades the working model was that as trees grow taller, gravity and vessel length create a hydraulic ceiling that limits water transport, photosynthesis, and growth — an elegant constraint that explained why trees don't just keep getting taller. A new study in Science, led by Exeter and Cardiff, found that giant Dipterocarp trees (some exceeding 80 meters) have evolved vessel architectures that fully compensate for height, leaving them no more drought-vulnerable than short trees of the same species. The infrastructure was there, quietly working, and we'd been modeling it wrong the whole time.
