The World This Week

Iran's Unfinished Succession — and What Comes Next
CBS News / Wikipedia / Stimson Center

Khamenei was killed in a joint US-Israeli strike on February 28, and today millions are attending his dayslong state funeral in Tehran — with peace talks between Iran and the US paused while the ceremony plays out. The succession has already been settled on paper: his son Mojtaba was named supreme leader in March, but analysts note the IRGC is the real power behind any throne, and the Stimson Center's framing is blunt: no significant policy shift can happen while bombs are still falling. The deeper question is whether whoever governs Iran next reads Khamenei's 'resistance economy' strategy as a failure worth abandoning — or a legacy worth defending.

Starlink Is Quietly Rewiring African Connectivity
The Economist

Across Africa, where terrestrial internet infrastructure has lagged for decades, Starlink is rapidly becoming the default broadband option for businesses, schools, and households. This is a geopolitical story as much as a tech one: satellite internet bypasses the fiber gatekeepers that African governments and Chinese state firms have historically controlled, shifting leverage toward users and away from centralized infrastructure owners. The Economist's piece is worth reading as a case study in how a single hardware product can quietly redistribute informational power at a continental scale.

The Circuit That Lets Your Brain Think and See at the Same Time
Columbia Engineering

Columbia researchers have identified a specific neural circuit that appears to mediate the link between visual perception and abstract thought — the mechanism by which what you're looking at gets woven into what you're thinking about. It's a piece of the brain architecture that explains, at the hardware level, why imagination and observation feel so entangled: they share physical substrate. For anyone who works visually — making things, drawing, building — this is a description of the machinery underneath the experience.

Alzheimer's May Start With Flexibility, Not Memory
Nautilus

New research suggests the earliest cognitive sign of Alzheimer's isn't forgetting — it's losing the ability to switch between mental tasks, what scientists call cognitive flexibility. Memory loss may be downstream of a more fundamental failure in the brain's ability to fluidly update its models of the world. This reframes early detection entirely: the first warning might not be 'I can't remember where I put my keys' but 'I can't seem to think about two things at once.'

Artificial Cell Divides — Briefly
Ars Technica

Scientists have built a synthetic cell capable of undergoing a few rounds of cell division — the closest thing yet to a truly artificial living system. It only works for several generations before running out of steam, and it requires a lot of externally supplied material to pull it off, so 'life' is a generous term. But the fact that it divides at all is the result: division is the behavior that distinguishes life from very sophisticated chemistry, and watching it happen in a cell you built is a different kind of moment.

History Worth Knowing

Amsterdam and the Invention of the Fire Department
Works in Progress

Works in Progress traces how 17th-century Amsterdam — a dense city of wooden buildings packed onto narrow canal lots — invented systematic firefighting out of sheer urban necessity, creating the institutional template that every modern fire department descends from. It's a great case study in how extreme material constraints (flammable buildings, no water mains, crowded geography) force institutional innovation that then spreads globally. The piece also quietly argues something broader: that most civic infrastructure we take for granted was invented once, somewhere specific, by people solving a concrete problem.

Making & Medicine

Reviving Dead Donor Eyes — The Unlock for Whole-Eye Transplants
MIT Technology Review

A new device can revive and preserve donor eyeballs that would normally degrade within hours of death, keeping them viable long enough to potentially enable whole-eye transplants — something surgeons have never been able to do successfully. The bottleneck has always been the eye's fragility outside the body; this device addresses that directly. It's early, but if it works at scale it would be transformative for the roughly 40 million people who are blind worldwide.

GLP-1 Peptides Are Becoming Cardiovascular Drugs — and the Regulatory Map Is Shifting
Proxiva Labs / FormBlends

GLP-1 agonists like semaglutide have shown a 20% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events in people without diabetes, fundamentally repositioning the entire class from diabetes drugs to metabolic-cardiovascular protectants. Separately, the FDA has opened a new public docket for therapeutic peptide review, including upcoming section 503A hearings on July 23-24 covering BPC-157, TB-500, KPV, and several others — meaning the compounding access landscape for peptides widely discussed in longevity circles is about to get much clearer (or more restricted) in the next few weeks. Worth watching closely if this is your space.
