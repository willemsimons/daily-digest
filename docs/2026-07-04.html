Biology doing something it shouldn't be able to do

Blind Cavefish Rewired the Same Neurons, Not New Ones
Florida Atlantic University / Science Advances

Blind Mexican cavefish (Astyanax mexicanus) became more active in light rather than darkness — the precise opposite of their sighted surface relatives — not by evolving new neural machinery, but by inverting the polarity of existing dopamine-related circuits: the same neurons that fire in darkness in surface fish now fire in light in cavefish. Whole-brain imaging at cellular resolution confirmed this isn't metaphor; it's a literal rewiring of inherited architecture. The researchers note implications for Parkinson's, ADHD, and schizophrenia, since those same dopamine pathways are implicated — meaning a fish that's been in the dark for hundreds of thousands of years may be illuminating human neurology.

The First Synthetic Cell Built Entirely Bottom-Up
Nautilus

The 'SpudCell,' announced by University of Minnesota researchers under biotech nonprofit Biotic, is a 90,000 base-pair system assembled from purified, non-living components — not a stripped-down living cell, but a from-scratch construction that has demonstrated feeding, DNA replication, and reproduction for a few generations. Unlike Venter's earlier minimal-cell work, which carved life down, this builds up from chemistry. The manuscript is not yet peer-reviewed, and Nautilus notes the split: the lead researcher says it proves life needs no 'magical spark,' while Imperial College's Yuval Elani says it's a real advance but explicitly not 'life created in the lab' — which is exactly the right tension to sit with.

Migratory Songbirds Navigate Thousands of Miles Solo, and We Now Know How
University of Exeter

Published in Science, this study used data-logger backpacks on pied flycatchers tracked from eight locations across Europe — Spain to Scandinavia — to show that solo, night-migrating songbirds follow consistent, non-direct routes to specific African wintering grounds, determined by a combination of genetics and local environmental conditions rather than social learning. They don't follow parents or companions; they arrive at the same destinations anyway. It's one of the cleanest demonstrations yet that complex navigational 'knowledge' can be encoded in biology without ever being taught.

Making things, and the systems around making

Bambu Lab Made More Money in 2025 Than the Three Top Industrial 3D Printing Companies Combined
VoxelMatters

VoxelMatters' 2026 Polymer AM Market Report puts the total polymer 3D printing market at $9.45 billion in 2025 — up 18.7% year over year — but the structural shock is that Bambu Lab, a consumer desktop brand that industrial analysts largely dismissed, generated more revenue than the three largest industrial polymer AM companies combined. Hardware was the biggest segment at $4.36 billion. The forecast to 2030 is $22 billion, but the real story is that the desktop-maker ecosystem, not the industrial tier, is now setting the pace.

Factories Are Just Rooms
Matt Webb / interconnected.org

Matt Webb went into his kid's school and talked about manufacturing — prototyping, Shenzhen factory floors, injection moulding vs. 3D printing, vibration testing, packaging design — and found it the most rewarding talk he'd given all year, mostly because seven-year-olds ask the right questions ('how does it not break in the post?'). Off the map

The Fall and Rise of Screwworm
Construction Physics

Brian Potter's deep dive covers one of the most astonishing eradication campaigns in history: the US wiped out screwworm — a flesh-eating parasite that was eating livestock alive across the entire American South — by breeding hundreds of millions of sterile male flies and dropping them by aircraft across an entire continent, overwhelming the native fertile population until the species effectively collapsed from within. The campaign worked so completely that most people alive today have never heard of screwworm. Now, as of June 2026, it's back in Texas and New Mexico — and the infrastructure that maintained the eradication barrier has been quietly fraying for years.
