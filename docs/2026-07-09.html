After Losing 24 Reapers, the Pentagon Bets on Drones Built to Be Shot Down
TechTimes / Defense Innovation Unit

The DIU's Massed Modular Aircraft solicitation is a doctrine-level inversion: instead of making platforms survivable, make them cheap enough that losing them is a tactical footnote. Twenty-four MQ-9 Reapers were destroyed in six weeks over Iran — at up to $50M each, that's $700M+ gone and the Air Force dropped below its own operational minimum. The MMA's government-owned open-architecture payload spec means no contractor lock-in, and the July 23 deadline suggests urgency bordering on panic.

The Glass Backbone: Why the Army's Logistics Will Break in the Next War
Modern War Institute at West Point

Two decades of optimizing for uncontested supply lines, contractor support, and static FOBs has left the US Army with a sustainment tail that's essentially a liability in any contested environment. West Point's war studies journal draws the through-line from Barbarossa — the Wehrmacht shattered Soviet lines then literally ran out of fuel and winter clothing because their logistics assumed a short campaign — to the current NDS pivot toward large-scale combat operations. The argument: a lethal maneuver force without a survivable logistics backbone is just a stationary target waiting to culminate.

Syria's Solar Boom: Infrastructure from Catastrophe
The National

Damascus is now the most solar-dense city in the Middle East — not by policy but because the grid delivers four hours of electricity a day and cheap Chinese panels filled the void. Syria's off-grid solar capacity went from 249MW in 2022 to 2,060MW last year, with a quarter of households running some form of solar, outpacing every regional neighbor except Lebanon and Jordan. It's a live case study in what bottom-up infrastructure looks like when the state simply stops providing: not planned resilience but accumulated desperation that accidentally becomes a model.

EU Chat Control 1.0 Passes — Against the Majority's Wishes
Patrick Breyer MEP

In a procedural maneuver that will sting for years, the EU Parliament let suspicionless mass scanning of private messages become law even though 314 MEPs voted against it and only 276 for — because a rejection required an absolute majority of 361, not a simple majority. Encrypted communications got a symbolic carve-out that means nothing in practice. This mandates client-side or server-side scanning across EU messenger platforms through 2028 and sets the baseline for the upcoming permanent regulation negotiation.

Bun Rewrites Itself in Rust
Bun.sh

Jarred Sumner built the original Bun — JavaScript runtime, bundler, package manager, test runner, and more — alone in a year in Zig, pre-LLM, in a cramped Oakland apartment. Now, post-Anthropic acquisition, the team is rewriting it in Rust, using Claude Fable 5 to do a significant chunk of the translation. The interesting tension: Zig made the first impossible sprint possible by offering low-level control with minimal friction, but at 22 million monthly downloads and real production bets from Claude Code and Vercel, the stability cost of Zig's immaturity became too high.

Amelia Cross's Sewn Paintings: Where Trompe-L'œil Meets Bespoke Tailoring
Colossal

Cross trained in bespoke tailoring at London College of Fashion before a painting MFA at the Royal College of Art, and the combination produced something genuinely strange: canvases where actual sewn pockets, structured collars, and fabric sit alongside painted trompe-l'œil shadows of the same, and you cannot immediately tell which is real. She thinks of the stretcher bars as a body she's dressing — the wooden armature as skeleton — which reframes the whole history of canvas support as secretly a garment problem. The perceptual confusion is the point: clothing already conceals and signals simultaneously.

Lalibela: Monumental Architecture Carved Downward from Solid Rock
Aeon Video

Ethiopia's medieval Lalibela churches invert every assumption about how buildings are made: instead of stacking material upward, 12th-century builders removed stone downward from a single living rock mass, leaving fully formed churches with windows, columns, and courtyards that were never assembled — only revealed. It's subtractive architecture at monumental scale, the logic of sculpture applied to cathedral-building, and it still isn't fully understood how they pulled it off.

Off the map

Yamanote.fun — A Complete Soundscape for Tokyo's Loop Line
Show HN

Someone built a complete ambient audio environment that maps every station melody, door chime, announcement, and train sound on the Yamanote Line — Tokyo's most-ridden commuter loop — playable in both clockwise and counterclockwise directions because each has its own distinct set of melodies. It runs at twice real-world speed so you can complete the full loop in 30 minutes. The entire thing is obsessive completionism in service of a single quiet idea: that a city's transit system has a sound identity as legible and emotional as its visual one.

'Worthless Idiot, Donkey Head': The Renaissance Pedant as Cultural Antibody
The Public Domain Review

The Pedant was a stock figure on the Italian Renaissance stage — not just a bore but a threat, someone whose display of learning was understood as a bid for social dominance that needed to be ritually humiliated. Arnoud Visser's essay tracks how the character encoded anxieties about humanist credentialism: the pedant didn't know too much, he knew in the wrong way, performing erudition as status rather than deploying it as understanding. Montaigne, surrounded by quotations from Greek and Latin, was acutely aware he was one misread gesture away from becoming the joke.

FILM
La Région Centrale
Michael Snow

A camera mounted on a custom-built mechanical armature sweeps, spins, and inverts across a barren Quebec landscape for three hours — the machine's choreography is the film. It's a generative 3D system made before the language existed, and staring at it long enough collapses the boundary between sculpture, code, and landscape.

MUSIC
I Am Sitting in a Room
Alvin Lucier

Lucier records his voice, plays it back into the room, records that, repeats — until the room's resonant frequencies consume the speech entirely. A feedback loop that makes acoustics visible through time. The room becomes the instrument, and the process is the composition.
