Die Casting Comes Home
Hackaday

A desktop CNC-milled graphite die, a hydraulic cylinder, and a pair of old trampoline springs: that's the entire injection system this builder used to produce die-cast aluminum parts at home. The same graphite serves as both the mold and the injector piston — he tested the process with molten wax first, then moved to aluminum. The detail and dimensional consistency of the output is industrial-grade, and the whole thing makes pressurized-molten-metal casting feel almost casual.

Brown University: In-Person Final, Scores Dropped 50%
Ars Technica

Economics professor Roberto Serrano switched to take-home exams after a campus shooting shook him — enrollment in his notoriously hard course immediately spiked. When he required an in-person final, scores fell by half. This isn't an AI story in the narrow sense; it's a story about what happens when a shortcut becomes load-bearing infrastructure for an entire cohort's academic performance, and what 'knowing something' even means anymore in a high-stakes environment full of people who are smart enough to have avoided having to learn things.

Phlegm: Copper Engraving, Dürer, and Murals Built Like Riddles
Colossal

Phlegm spent the last two years in near-monastic study of Albrecht Dürer's 'Knight, Death, and the Devil' — collecting old prints, examining them under a microscope, reverse-engineering the line technique — before engraving his own version where every element is replaced with his own iconography while preserving Dürer's exact compositional logic and mark-making approach. He describes his work as floating in a 'grey area' where meaning can't fully cross the barrier between people, which is precisely what makes the images function as mythology rather than illustration.

How Light 'Knows' the Shortest Path
Veritasium

Fermat's principle says light always takes the path of least time — but how does it 'know' in advance which path that is? Veritasium uses this as a door into Feynman's path integral formulation: light doesn't pick one path, it takes all of them simultaneously, and the non-extremal paths cancel each other out through interference. The question sounds like philosophy and lands squarely in the foundations of quantum mechanics.

Off the map

Hunting a Novel Psychedelic in the Mushroom Behind Lilliputian Hallucinations
Nautilus

Mushrooms from the genus Lanmaoa are the only known biological trigger for Lilliputian hallucinations — the experience of watching hordes of tiny, vivid, friendly miniature people moving around your actual furniture. Researchers at the University of Utah just built the first family tree for the genus and found it contains none of the genes for psilocybin or ibotenic acid, the two known psychedelic compounds in fungi. Whatever is causing these visions is chemically novel, and hasn't been identified yet — meaning there's an entirely unknown psychedelic out there, encoded in a mushroom, doing something to the brain that nothing else does.

Happening in New York

Nearly 300 photographs and archival objects tracing how YSL used photography not just for promotion but as a system for constructing identity, myth, and cultural authority — with work by Avedon, Newton, Penn, Warhol, and Guy Bourdin alongside contact sheets and campaign materials that show the machinery behind the legend.

Pierre Huyghe: UUmwelt
MoMA Abby Aldrich Rockefeller Sculpture Garden, 11 W 53rd St, Manhattan · Jul 1 – Nov 29

Huyghe placed freestanding screens in MoMA's Sculpture Garden running a machine-learning system trained on fMRI scans of a person imagining images — the work activates in real time from visitor gazes and cancer-cell mutation data, making the boundary between human and machine cognition the actual medium; it's as close to a living artwork as a museum has shown.

Make something

Build a small WebGL sketch where light rays pathfind across a scene — not by tracing the correct path, but by visibly simulating many paths simultaneously and letting the 'wrong' ones fade out through destructive interference. The Veritasium piece frames this as the actual physics: light takes all paths and cancels itself except at the extremum. Make the cancellation visible. Even a 2D grid of wave amplitudes summing and colliding would do it — the goal is to see Fermat's principle emerge from chaos rather than be stated.
