I.J. Good's 1964 Paper on the First Ultraintelligent Machine
Hackaday

Good's thesis — 'the survival of man depends on the early construction of an ultraintelligent machine' — was written at Oxford before integrated circuits were common, and his core chain (a smart machine designs a smarter machine, recursively) is exactly the argument being re-litigated right now. He also noted in 1964 that we'd need to understand the human brain to make a real breakthrough, and we still don't — which either means LLMs are something else entirely or we're pattern-matching our way toward something we can't fully see. Reading present-day fears through 60-year-old speculation has a clarifying effect.

Letters, marks, and the physical act of making

From Bull's Head to 'A' — How an Ox Became the Alphabet
MyFonts

The Latin 'A' is a stylized ox head rotated 90 degrees: aleph, drawn by Bronze Age traders in the Sinai, had two horns and a triangular face — tip it over, add a crossbar to hold the diagonals apart, and you have the skeleton of every 'A' you've ever typed. The rotation wasn't aesthetic; it followed the shift from carving into stone (where vertical strokes dominate) to writing on papyrus (where horizontal motion is faster). What's quietly remarkable is the chain: Phoenician abjad → Greek vowel reassignment (a cognitive leap the essay calls 'ergonomic') → Etruscan → Roman stonecutters rationalizing the form for chisels.

Backtrack-Free Cursive — Engineering the One-Stroke Word
mmapped.blog

The author noticed that English cursive requires backtracking — lifting or reversing to dot i's and cross t's — in 51% of words, averaging 0.68 backtracks per word, while Russian needs it in only 6.4% of words (0.066 per word), a difference he quantified by running Crime and Punishment through both languages. The essay is ostensibly about designing a pen-path algorithm for a backtrack-free cursive system, but it's really forcing you to think about how letterforms encode sequence and connection — the hidden temporal logic inside a static visual form.

SFPD Drone Footage Leak — What a City Looks Like When the Watchers Aren't Watching Themselves
Wired
