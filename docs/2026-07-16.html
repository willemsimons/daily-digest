Biology at the edge

Gene-Edited Islets: One T1D Patient, 14+ Months, No Immunosuppressants
Breakthrough T1D / NEJM

Sana Biotechnology's gene-edited donor islets — engineered to evade the immune system — have kept a single Type 1 diabetes patient producing their own insulin for over 14 months post-transplant, without any immunosuppressant drugs. That last part is the mechanism worth watching: the historical barrier to islet transplants has always been lifelong immunosuppression, not the islets themselves. One patient, early data, but the biology is doing something it wasn't supposed to be able to do.

Therapeutic Peptides Against the 12 Hallmarks of Aging — Frontiers Review
Frontiers in Aging

A peer-reviewed narrative review sweeping PubMed and Scopus through January 2026 maps nine peptides — tirzepatide, epitalon, GHK-Cu, BPC-157, TB-500, Semax, CJC-1295, ipamorelin, bremelanotide — against the established 12 hallmarks of aging framework. It's the most rigorous primary reference available in this space right now, and it draws a sharp line between FDA-approved agents with robust trial data and the non-approved peptides where the evidence is still preclinical or early-phase.

Platforms cracking, culture shifting

Third-Party App Stores Are Coming to Google Play Next Week
Ars Technica

The Epic-Google settlement is dead, which means Judge Donato's full antitrust remedies kick in — including Google being required to host rival app stores directly inside Google Play. This is a structurally different outcome than Apple's case, where Epic got almost nothing; here the platform is being forced to distribute its own competition. Indie developers and alternative ecosystems now have a real distribution path on Android that didn't exist a month ago.

Pigeons & Planes

Rob Sheridan — the Nine Inch Nails creative director who also accidentally created the dancing baby meme — anchors this essay on what What.CD and Oink actually were: not just illegal file sharing but elaborate social ecosystems where scarcity, curation, and community created taste in ways Spotify's frictionless abundance structurally cannot. Off the map

Archie G. Norcross' Maine Forest Fire Maps (1918–22)
Public Domain Review

These circular maps were designed for fire tower lookouts — the geometry of the map literally mirrors the geometry of a human body rotating 360 degrees in place, so the tool fits the body fits the landscape. Kerouac spent a summer in one of these towers (broke, pre-On The Road, chasing Gary Snyder's ascetic ideal) and the essay uses that as a way into what it meant to be a person whose entire job was vigilant looking. Information design as embodied instrument, from 1918.

Applied Science: Cross-Sectioning Insects With a Femtosecond Laser Inside an Electron Microscope
YouTube — Applied Science

Applied Science uses a femtosecond laser — pulses so short they ablate material before heat can diffuse — as a surgical scalpel to cross-section insects mid-body, then images the exposed internal structures with a scanning electron microscope. This channel sits at the exact intersection of obsessive craft and unreasonable technical depth; the combination of imaging modalities here (ultrafast laser + SEM) is something most university labs don't have running together.

For you

FILM
Decasia
Bill Morrison

Nitrate film stock dissolving in real time — the decay IS the image, not a flaw in it. Morrison found footage of the physical substrate of cinema collapsing into abstract forms; it's a film about entropy as generative system, and it will make you want to build something that thinks about material degradation the way WebGL thinks about light.

ART
Haim Steinbach: once again the world is flat
Haim Steinbach

Steinbach spent decades arranging commercial objects on formica shelves and calling it sculpture — which sounds thin until you realize he was building an exact anthropology of desire, status, and the invisible grammar that makes certain objects feel sacred. The work is about the systems underneath consumer culture, made visible by the obsessive neutrality of the gesture.

MUSIC
Big Science
Laurie Anderson

The 1982 album that turned spoken language, vocoder mythology, and field recording into a coherent emotional world — art functioning as community ritual, technology as feeling, self as construction project. 'O Superman' alone is a complete theory of infrastructure and longing compressed into eight minutes.
